Accessibility

Universal accessibility

Our website, as our final assignment, should have the same look and layout for the first four pages and ensure universal accessibility. While in the last page, the fun page, we could freely let our creativity loose and explore our ideas.

Preparation

Before we started, we agreed to make the general layout and structure of the web pages as simple and clean as possible, but also aesthetically pleasing. We explored all our ideas, and looked at different websites for inspiration, and then decided on our layout and structure based on what was the most aesthetically pleasing and clean.

The menu

Our first point was to make it easy to navigate between the pages, thus, we had our menu fixed on top available to switch between the pages at any time. At first, we had our header fixed on the left for our main pages (figure 1) because we wanted to make it somewhat new and refreshing, and we could get more room from top to bottom for the main content, but we thought that some people with reading disabilities or visual impairments might have some difficulties with that as the title was rotated vertically, so we saved our idea for our fun page instead where we could let our creativity loose.

Screen readers

Another important group of people that we focused on was people that use screen readers. It was important that we used all the semantic tags available such as <nav>, <header>, <main>, <footer>, <figure> and <em> instead of <i> for instance. We also made sure we used correct hierarchy of headings, and to have alternative texts explaining the image as detailed as possible for our important images. We also validated our html and css codes regularly to make sure everything was free for errors (figure 2).

Other points

Some other things we paid attention to was if the text and images had sufficient color contrast (figure 3), and if we avoided the color combinations that should be avoided. We also tested ourselves if it was possible to navigate our website through the keyboard only. On smaller screens we have a hamburger icon with a “menu”-label for our navigation in case some might not know that it is a menu. We also avoided javascript to make the website generally more accessible.

Accessibility check

Once we made sure to use semantic tags, correct hierarchy and paid attention to text and image contrast, we ran our code through an accessibility check. This is a necessary and useful step that can help us detect potential accessibility issues that can arise. Thankfully because we were conscious of accessibility; the check showed no issues (figure 4). We ran each web page through the accessibility checks and got the same result.

Summary

There were things that we had to keep in the back of our mind when we started the assignment. We started coding with a rough idea of how we wanted our page to look. Of course, this changed further down the development road as we had to think of accessibility. We also made sure to accommodate different screen sizes. To conclude, we were happy with our website.
